When to Upgrade Your Electrical Panel

Your panel was sized for the home’s original loads — not for today’s. Here’s how to know when it’s time to upgrade and what the process looks like.

Is Your Panel Keeping Up?

The electrical panel was sized when your home was built — for the loads that existed then. Over the decades, the average home’s electrical demand has increased dramatically. Air conditioning, EV chargers, heat pumps, home offices, and smart home systems were never planned for in a 1960s 100A panel. If your panel is original and your home has grown, it’s worth a look.

Clear Signs You Need an Upgrade

Frequent breaker trips. If you regularly reset breakers on multiple circuits, your panel may not have the capacity your home needs.

No room to add circuits. If every slot in your panel is filled, you cannot add new circuits for upgrades or additions without first adding capacity.

You want to add an EV charger. A Level 2 EV charger requires a dedicated 40–50A 240V circuit. Many older panels cannot support this without an upgrade to both the panel and service entrance.

You’re planning a major remodel. A kitchen remodel, basement finish, or home addition almost always requires additional circuits. If your panel is at capacity, the remodel triggers an upgrade.

Your panel is a known problem brand. Federal Pacific Electric (Stab-Lok), Zinsco, and Pushmatic panels have documented failure and fire risks. If you have one, replace it — don’t wait for something to fail.

You have 100A service. Modern all-electric homes, homes with EV chargers, or homes with heat pumps typically need 200A service. If you’re on 100A and planning any major electrical addition, the service upgrade is part of the project.

100A vs. 200A: What’s the Difference?

A 100A panel provides a maximum of 24,000 watts of available capacity (100A × 240V). In practice you’d never run it all simultaneously, but with a modern home’s demands you can get close. A 200A panel provides 48,000 watts and is the current standard for all new residential construction. Going from 100A to 200A typically requires a new panel, an upgraded main breaker, and coordination with the utility to upgrade the service entrance wiring.

The Process in Minnesota

A panel upgrade requires an electrical permit, coordination with the utility for a temporary service disconnect during service entrance work, installation of the new panel and breakers, and inspection by the city or county inspector. In Minnesota, all of this work must be done by a licensed electrician. The permit and inspection process ensures the work is documented and meets code — which also matters when you sell the home.

What About a Sub-Panel?

If your main panel is in good shape but you need additional circuits for a garage, workshop, or addition, a sub-panel is often a cost-effective solution. The sub-panel is fed from the main panel and provides its own circuit slots for the new load — without requiring a full service upgrade in many cases.
