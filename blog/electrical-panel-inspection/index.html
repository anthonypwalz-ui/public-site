How Often Your Electrical Panel Should Be Inspected

Your breaker panel controls every circuit in your home — but most homeowners never open the door. Here’s what to look for and how often to look.

Your Panel Is the Heart of Your Home’s Electrical System

The electrical panel — your breaker box — controls every circuit in your home. But many homeowners only open the door to reset a tripped breaker. Like a furnace filter or a roof, panels need periodic attention. Problems that go unnoticed inside a panel can develop silently for years before causing a failure or fire.

Recommended Inspection Frequency

Every 10 years for a typical residential panel in a home without major electrical additions or concerns.

Every 5 years if the home is older than 40 years, has had repeated electrical issues, has a panel brand known for problems, or has had significant new loads added such as an EV charger, hot tub, or major addition.

Immediately if you notice any of the warning signs described below.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Rust or corrosion. Rust inside a panel indicates moisture infiltration — a serious problem that can cause shorts, breaker failure, and arcing. Minnesota homes with panels in unfinished basements or detached garages are particularly susceptible due to seasonal humidity swings.

Breakers that feel loose or won’t stay on. Breaker mechanisms wear out. A breaker that won’t stay in the on position, or that trips at very low loads, may have failed internally and should be replaced.

Burn marks or melted plastic inside the panel. Any sign of heat damage is an emergency. Something has already overheated inside the panel.

Buzzing, humming, or crackling sounds. A properly functioning panel should be nearly silent. Electrical sounds indicate arcing or a loose connection somewhere inside.

Warm panel door. Like a warm outlet, a warm panel door means heat is being generated inside the enclosure.

Outdated panel brand. Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok panels and Zinsco panels have well-documented failure rates and are considered unsafe by most electricians and insurers. If you have one, get it replaced rather than waiting for something to fail.

What an Inspection Includes

A qualified electrician will check for correct breaker ratings versus wire gauge, signs of overheating or moisture, double-tapped breakers (two wires on one breaker terminal, a code violation unless the breaker is rated for it), missing knockouts that leave the interior exposed, proper grounding, and whether panel capacity matches the home’s current electrical load.

When Inspection Turns Into Replacement

If the panel is over 25–30 years old, is undersized for current loads, or has any serious warning signs, replacement is often more cost-effective than repeated repairs. A new 200A panel also future-proofs the home for EV chargers, solar, and other additions that are common in modern Twin Cities homes.
