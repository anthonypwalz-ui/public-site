Flickering or Dimming Lights

Some light flicker is harmless. Some indicates wiring faults that can damage electronics and appliances. Here’s how to tell the difference.

Not All Flicker Is Created Equal

Light flicker falls into two categories: annoying but harmless, and a symptom of a real electrical problem. Knowing the difference matters both for your safety and for your appliances.

Harmless Causes

LED compatibility issues. Some LED bulbs flicker when used with older dimmers that weren’t designed for LEDs. Switching to an LED-compatible dimmer or a fully dimmable LED bulb usually resolves this completely.

Appliance startup. High-draw motors — refrigerators, HVAC units, washing machines — pull extra current when starting up. A brief flicker when one of these kicks on is normal, as long as it stops within a second or two.

Causes That Need Attention

Loose or failing neutral connection. The neutral wire is the return path for current. A loose neutral anywhere in the circuit — at the outlet, junction box, or panel — creates an unstable circuit. Lights flicker erratically and the voltage on the circuit becomes unpredictable, which can damage electronics and appliances.

Overloaded circuit. If lights flicker whenever a particular appliance runs, the circuit may be undersized for the combined load. The voltage drop as more current is pulled causes visible dimming.

Loose wiring connections. Backstab outlets, old push-in connectors, or loosened terminal screws create inconsistent current flow. Lights flicker as the connection makes and breaks under load.

Voltage fluctuations at the service entrance. If lights all over the house flicker simultaneously, the problem may be at the utility connection or main panel. This is a situation for either the utility company or a licensed electrician.

The Risk in Ignoring It

A loose neutral is particularly dangerous. It can cause uneven voltage distribution across the house, sending overvoltage to one leg of the circuit while leaving another with undervoltage. This can destroy appliances, damage electronics, and in severe cases, cause overheating inside walls.

What to Tell Your Electrician

Note when the flickering happens: Is it random, when a specific appliance runs, or constant? Is it one room or the whole house? Does it happen more at certain times of day? This information helps a technician diagnose the issue much faster and saves time on the service call.
