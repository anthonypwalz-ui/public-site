Why Lights Flicker When Appliances Turn On

A brief dip when the fridge kicks on is usually nothing. Lingering or widespread dimming is telling you something else. Here’s how to read the difference.

A Brief Flicker When Your Fridge Kicks On — Normal or Not?

A very brief, single dimming event when a large appliance starts is generally within normal range. High-draw motors — refrigerators, HVAC systems, washing machines, sump pumps — pull significantly more current at startup than during normal operation. This momentary surge can cause a slight voltage drop on the circuit, which shows up as a brief dimming of lights.

The key word is brief. A flicker lasting less than half a second when an appliance starts is usually not concerning.

When It’s Not Normal

The flicker lasts more than a moment. If lights dim for several seconds each time an appliance starts, the circuit is likely undersized for the combined load it’s carrying.

Flickering happens on circuits other than where the appliance is. If kitchen lights dim when a bedroom AC unit starts, the issue is at the panel or service entrance level — the entire home is experiencing a voltage drop.

Multiple different appliances all cause flickering. If nearly any heavy appliance triggers light dimming, your service capacity may be undersized for your home’s current load.

Flickering happens without any appliance starting. Random flickering unrelated to appliance cycles is a different problem entirely — typically loose connections or a failing neutral that needs immediate attention.

Root Causes Worth Knowing

Undersized circuit. Dedicated high-draw circuits should have enough headroom that startup surges don’t cause significant voltage drops elsewhere. If an appliance and lighting share a circuit, separating them usually resolves the issue.

100A service entrance. Many Twin Cities homes built before 1970 have 100A service — often insufficient for a fully modern home with air conditioning, an EV charger, and modern kitchen appliances. A service upgrade to 200A resolves persistent whole-house voltage fluctuations.

Loose panel connections. A loose bus bar connection or a loose main breaker inside the panel causes voltage fluctuations every time high current is drawn. This can be a fire risk in addition to causing annoying flicker.

The Fix

For appliance-related flickering, the most reliable solution is a dedicated circuit for the appliance in question. A licensed electrician can determine whether the issue is the circuit, the panel capacity, or the service entrance — and recommend the right solution rather than the most expensive one.
