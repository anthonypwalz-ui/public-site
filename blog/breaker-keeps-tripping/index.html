Breaker Keeps Tripping

A breaker trip is a signal, not just an inconvenience. Here’s what’s causing it and when you need to stop resetting it.

Why Do Breakers Trip?

Circuit breakers have one job: interrupt the flow of electricity when something goes wrong. They trip in response to three conditions:

Overload. The combined load on the circuit exceeds the breaker’s rating (15A, 20A, etc.). This is the most common cause. A hair dryer and a space heater on the same circuit is a quick overload.

Short circuit. A hot (live) wire contacts a neutral wire or a grounded surface. The resulting current spike is enormous and the breaker trips immediately. This is more serious than an overload.

Ground fault. Similar to a short, but the current is flowing to ground unintentionally — through a person, water, or a damaged appliance.

Is Resetting Safe?

Resetting a tripped breaker once is generally safe if you identify and address the cause first. Turn off or unplug whatever was running on the circuit, reset the breaker, and bring devices back one at a time.

What is not safe: repeatedly resetting a breaker that trips on the same circuit without figuring out why. A breaker that trips every time it’s reset is telling you something is seriously wrong.

Signs of a More Serious Problem

  • The breaker trips immediately when you reset it
  • The breaker feels warm or hot to the touch
  • You smell burning near the panel
  • The breaker trips with minimal load (just a single lamp, for example)
  • The panel is an older brand: Federal Pacific, Zinsco, or Pushmatic — all known for elevated failure rates in older Twin Cities homes

Minnesota Homes and Electrical Load

Many Twin Cities homes built in the 1950s through 1970s were wired for 15A circuits throughout. Modern refrigerators, microwaves, and especially HVAC equipment draw far more than the appliances those homes were designed for. A breaker that worked fine for 40 years may now trip regularly because the home’s electrical demand has simply outgrown its wiring.

Adding dedicated circuits for high-draw appliances — rather than sharing circuits — is often the right long-term fix.

When to Replace the Breaker

Breakers wear out. After thousands of trips and resets, the internal mechanism fatigues. A breaker that trips at lower-than-rated loads, or that feels loose in the panel, should be replaced. This is not a DIY job — working inside a panel involves lethal voltage even with the main breaker off.
