Outlet Keeps Popping or Tripping

Repeated tripping is a signal, not a glitch. Here’s what’s causing it and what you should do.

What Does It Mean When an Outlet “Trips”?

Most outlets in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, and basements have a built-in GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter). This device monitors the current flowing through the circuit in real time. When it detects a difference between the outgoing and returning current — even as small as 5 milliamps — it shuts off instantly to prevent shock.

That’s a good thing. But if your outlet is tripping repeatedly, something is causing it to trigger.

Common Reasons Your Outlet Keeps Tripping

Overloaded circuit. Plugging too many high-draw devices into the same circuit — hair dryers, space heaters, power tools — can push the circuit beyond its rated capacity. The GFCI trips as a safety response.

Moisture and humidity. In Minnesota, basements and garages deal with significant humidity swings between seasons. Even small amounts of moisture inside an outlet box can cause leakage current that trips a GFCI. If your basement outlet trips every spring when humidity rises, moisture is the likely culprit.

Worn or aging outlets. Older receptacles degrade internally. The contacts loosen, the plastic housing cracks, and the internal sensing components drift out of calibration — causing nuisance trips.

Faulty appliance or device. An appliance with an internal ground fault will trip a GFCI outlet every time. Test the outlet with a different device before assuming the outlet itself is the problem.

Wiring fault. Loose connections, deteriorating insulation, or incorrectly wired outlets — especially reversed neutral/ground wires — can cause repeated tripping.

When Is Resetting Safe?

If the outlet tripped once and resets cleanly, it likely detected a real fault and did its job. Unplug everything on the circuit, press the reset button, and plug devices back in one at a time to find the culprit.

It is not safe to repeatedly reset an outlet that keeps tripping without identifying the cause. Repeated resetting without diagnosis masks the underlying problem and increases the risk of electrical fire or shock.

Warning Signs That Warrant a Professional

  • The outlet trips immediately when you press reset
  • The outlet is warm or discolored
  • You smell burning near the outlet
  • The outlet is in a basement, bathroom, or garage and trips regularly in humid conditions
  • Multiple outlets on the same circuit are tripping
  • The outlet has visible physical damage

Minnesota-Specific Concerns

Older Twin Cities homes — particularly those built before 1970 — often have wiring systems that weren’t designed for modern electrical loads. A GFCI that trips in an older home may be revealing a wiring deficiency that has been there for decades. Additionally, basement workshops and garages in Minnesota homes frequently have circuits that are undersized for the tools and equipment now being used on them.

What a Technician Will Check

A licensed electrician will check the GFCI device itself, inspect all outlets on the circuit, test for ground faults using a dedicated meter, check for moisture intrusion, and examine the wiring connections in the box. In many cases, a simple outlet replacement resolves the issue. In others, a circuit extension or breaker replacement is needed.
