Electrical FAQ

What Is an Arc Fault and Why It Matters

Arc faults generate intense heat inside your walls without triggering a standard breaker. They’re a leading cause of home fires — and most homes built before 2014 have no protection against them.

Arcing: The Silent Fire Starter

Electrical arcing occurs when current jumps across a gap — through air, through degraded insulation, or across loose connections. The temperature of an electrical arc can exceed 35,000°F. When this happens inside a wall cavity surrounded by wood framing and insulation, the results can be catastrophic and often happen without any visible sign until a fire is already underway.

How Arc Faults Happen

Damaged wire insulation. Nails driven through walls during renovation, staples applied too tightly during original installation, or insulation damaged by pests — any of these create a spot where current can jump the gap and arc.

Loose connections. At outlets, switches, junction boxes, or the panel itself, loose terminals allow small arcs to occur every time current flows. This is especially common in homes with backstab outlets or aged push-in wire connectors.

Pinched or damaged cords. Extension cords and appliance cords run under rugs, pinched by furniture, or bent at tight angles develop internal damage that causes arcing at the point of stress.

Aged or faulty appliances. Internal wiring in appliances can degrade and begin arcing without any external sign. Old lamps, power tools, and kitchen appliances are common sources.

Why Regular Breakers Don’t Catch It

Standard circuit breakers trip on overcurrent — too much amperage flowing through the circuit. An arc fault may not create enough additional current to trip a standard breaker. The arc can persist for a long time, generating enormous heat, without ever activating the overcurrent protection. This is why arc faults are responsible for an estimated 30,000 home fires annually in the United States.

What AFCI Protection Does

AFCI (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter) breakers and outlets monitor the electrical “signature” of current on the circuit — looking for the distinctive waveform pattern that arc faults produce. They can detect dangerous arcing and shut off the circuit in milliseconds, before enough heat builds to start a fire.

Since 2014, the National Electrical Code has required AFCI protection on most circuits in new residential construction. Older homes almost certainly don’t have this protection unless it has been specifically added.

Should You Upgrade to AFCI?

If your home was built before 2014 and hasn’t had major electrical work, your circuits almost certainly lack AFCI protection. The upgrade is straightforward — AFCI breakers replace standard breakers in the panel, one circuit at a time. For older homes where degraded wiring and aging appliances make arc faults more statistically likely, this upgrade is one of the most impactful safety investments available.
